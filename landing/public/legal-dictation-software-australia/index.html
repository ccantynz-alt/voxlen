FAQ

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers to common questions from Australian legal professionals.

Is Voxlen AI compliant with the Australian Privacy Act?
Yes. Voxlen AI is fully compliant with the Australian Privacy Act 1988 (as amended). Voxlen never stores your audio or transcripts — requests pass through zero-retention endpoints straight to the AI providers, and nothing is retained. Session history and custom vocabulary stay on your machine. Fully on-device transcription via a local Whisper engine (Privileged Mode) is coming soon for client-related work requiring absolute isolation.
Does Voxlen AI understand Australian legal terminology?
Voxlen's on-device flywheel learns your specific legal vocabulary over time — including Australian courts (Federal Court, Family Court of Australia, Supreme Court), regulatory bodies like ASIC and APRA, statutes, and procedure-specific terminology. The more you use it, the more accurate it becomes for your practice area. You can also import a custom vocabulary list at onboarding.
Can I use Voxlen for Federal Court and Family Court filings?
Absolutely. Voxlen AI is designed for the full range of Australian legal work — from drafting Federal Court pleadings and Family Court affidavits to ASIC regulatory correspondence and Australian Bar Association submissions. The AI understands Australian procedural language and formats output accordingly.
How accurate is Voxlen AI for legal dictation?
Voxlen achieves over 98% word-level accuracy on legal content using Deepgram Nova-2 for streaming transcription. Combined with AI grammar correction via Claude or GPT-4o-mini, output is near publication-ready. Accuracy improves further as the system learns your voice, accent, and vocabulary over time.
What platforms does Voxlen AI support?
Voxlen AI is available as a desktop app for macOS and Windows, and as an iOS keyboard extension for dictation on iPhone and iPad. Text is injected directly into any application — Word, Leap, Affinity, email clients, or your firm's practice management software — using native OS-level keyboard simulation. No clipboard required.
